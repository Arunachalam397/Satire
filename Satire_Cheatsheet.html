§1 — What Satire Is (Memorise This Definition)

SATIRE is a literary/artistic mode that uses humour, irony, exaggeration, and ridicule to critique, expose, and mock the flaws, vices, and hypocrisies of individuals, institutions, or society — with the goal of prompting change or awareness.

The 3 Core Purposes of Satire

  • Expose — reveal what is hidden or denied
  • Mock — ridicule the powerful/absurd
  • Reform — urge the audience to think/act differently

The Golden Rule of Satire Analysis

Never just say "the author mocks X." Always explain HOW the technique works and WHY it positions the audience to see X as absurd, hypocritical, or wrong.

§2 — Satirical Techniques: Full Reference Bank

Memorise the name, one-line definition, and exam-ready example for each. These are your building blocks for every answer.

Language / Literary Techniques

Irony

A gap between what is said and what is meant, or between appearance and reality — used to highlight absurdity or hypocrisy.

Candide's castle is "grand" because it has "a door and windows" — the claim is grand, the reality is trivial.

Verbal Irony

Saying the opposite of what you mean to draw attention to the truth.

Sassoon's officer tells the mother her son died "bravely" when he actually panicked and fled — the lie exposes the glorification myth.

Situational Irony

When the outcome is the opposite of what is expected or appropriate.

The French King's power over money comes not from gold but from "the vanity of his subjects" — a ruler relying on foolishness rather than strength.

Hyperbole / Exaggeration

Extreme overstatement to make something appear ridiculous or dangerous.

Toles' tornado looming over the pig-figures while they deny climate change is happening — the threat is massively exaggerated visually.

Bathos

A sudden, jarring drop from the grand/serious to the trivial/mundane — deflates pretension.

The Baron celebrated as a great lord... because his castle has "a door and windows."

Understatement

Deliberately downplaying something serious to make the reality hit harder by contrast.

"It's all under control... we're having a meeting in November" — said while the landscape is a barren wasteland.

Sarcasm

Harsh or cutting irony, often with contempt — the tone makes the mockery clear.

The "prodigy of human pride" funding the King's army — "prodigy" ironically frames gullibility as an achievement.

Juxtaposition

Placing two contrasting ideas/images side by side to highlight the gap between them.

In "The Hero," "glorious boy" is juxtaposed with "cold-footed, useless swine" — same person, exposing the lie.

Parody

Imitating a style, genre, or form to mock it by exaggerating its features.

The Persian Letters parodies the travel letter genre — the "naive outsider" perspective makes European customs look absurd.

Stereotype

An exaggerated, reductive representation of a type of person used to mock that group's values or behaviour.

Parsons is the stereotypical Party loyalist — obsessively compliant, proud of mediocrity, unthinkingly devoted.

Oxymoron

Two contradictory words placed together to highlight an internal contradiction or absurdity.

"Prodigy of human pride" — a prodigy is remarkable talent, but here it is for being exploited and vain.

Allusion

A reference to a well-known text, story, or event that adds a layer of meaning.

Toles' three pigs allude to The Three Little Pigs — even the "brick house" (strongest denial) will fall.

Imagery / Metaphor

Figurative language that creates a vivid picture to convey an idea with emotional force.

The children in 1984 are like "gambolling tiger cubs... which will soon grow into man-eaters" — cute now, dangerous later.

Simile

A direct comparison using "like" or "as" to illuminate an idea vividly.

"Like the gambolling of tiger cubs which will soon grow up into man-eaters" — innocent appearance, predatory future.

Persona / Mask

A fictional narrator adopted by the author to create distance and allow critique from an "outsider" view.

Montesquieu uses Rica, a Persian outsider, so that French customs appear strange, irrational, and absurd.

Visual Techniques (Cartoons, Images)

Salience

The dominant visual element — what the eye is drawn to first — carries the central message.

In Toles, the massive tornado dominates the image, making the figures' denial look absurdly small by comparison.

Vector

Lines, gestures, or gaze that direct the viewer's eye from one element to another, guiding meaning.

The bottom-left to top-right reading path in Toles guides us through escalating excuses toward the tornado.

Scale / Size

Making something larger or smaller than reality to convey relative importance or threat.

In The River Bed, the landscape dwarfs the human figures — the scale of the crisis far outweighs human response.

Symbolism (Visual)

An image that represents a larger abstract idea.

The dried, cracked riverbed symbolises irreversible environmental collapse — no water = no life.

Juxtaposition (Visual)

Placing contrasting images side by side to highlight contradiction.

Barren wasteland + suited bureaucrats calmly "having a meeting" — the visual gap creates the satire.

Caricature

Exaggerating physical features to mock or ridicule a character or type.

The pig-like figures in Toles caricature politicians/public as greedy, stubborn animals burying their heads.

Caption / Labels

Written text within a cartoon that adds ironic or satirical commentary.

The escalating captions: "Climate change isn't real" → "It won't come soon" → "It won't be that bad" track the stages of denial.

Speech Bubble / Dialogue

Direct speech assigned to figures that reveals satirical irony through what is said vs. the visual context.

"It's all under control... we're having a meeting in November" spoken against a ravaged landscape.

Colour / Tone

Use of colour or black-and-white to create mood, emphasis, or contrast.

The barren brown tones of The River Bed create a bleak, hopeless atmosphere that amplifies the satire of inaction.

§4 — The Universal Answer Formula (CTEQ+)

Every paragraph you write must do these things in order:

  1. CONCEPT — Name the satirical target (what is being mocked: class, denial, war glory, political power, blind obedience)
  2. TECHNIQUE — Name the technique the composer uses
  3. EVIDENCE — Give the quote or visual detail (be specific and accurate)
  4. EXPLAIN — Explain how the technique creates satirical meaning. What does it reveal? What does it make the audience think/feel?
  5. EFFECT/PURPOSE — Zoom out: what is the composer ultimately critiquing? Position audience to laugh at? Question? Realise?

Single Text Paragraph Template

In [Text Title], [composer] uses [technique] to satirise [target/value]. The [quote/visual detail] [explains what it does], exposing [the flaw/hypocrisy]. This positions the audience to [realise / question / feel superior to] [what], critiquing [broader societal issue].

Comparative Paragraph Template

Both [Text 1] and [Text 2] use satire to critique [shared target]. In [Text 1], [technique] is used: [quote/detail + explain]. [Similarly / In contrast], [Text 2] employs [technique]: [detail + explain]. Together, both texts [shared satirical purpose], however [key difference].

§6 — Your Actual Exam Texts: Pre-Loaded Key Quotes & Analysis

These are the four texts from your practice exam — the real exam will have similar types. Memorise the approach for each text type.

Text 1 — Cartoons: 'Climate Change' (Toles, 2005) & 'The River Bed' (Tandberg)

Toles — 'Climate Change'

Satirical Target: Public and political denial of climate change

Key Visual Details:

  • Three pig-like figures digging holes as tornado rages overhead
  • Escalating caption signs: "Climate change isn't real" → "It won't come soon" → "It won't be that bad"
  • Caption at bottom: "Let's dig a hole! And keep digging!"
  • Tornado = inevitable consequence; pigs = society's denial

Key Techniques: Irony (denying visible disaster), allusion (Three Little Pigs — even the strongest denial falls), caricature (pig-figures = greedy/stubborn deniers), vector (bottom-left → top-right escalation), visual symbolism (digging deeper = making it worse)

Tandberg — 'The River Bed'

Satirical Target: Bureaucratic / political inaction and complacency

Key Visual Details:

  • Vast cracked, barren, dried-out landscape — barely a puddle left
  • Dead/dying trees throughout
  • Tiny suited figures with speech bubble: "It's all under control... we're having a meeting in November"
  • Scale: landscape dominates, people are tiny

Key Techniques: Understatement / sarcasm ("all under control"), juxtaposition (catastrophic landscape vs. bureaucratic calm), scale (dwarfed figures = human irrelevance/powerlessness), visual symbolism (dried river = death of environment), irony ("meeting in November" = far too late)

The KEY COMPARISON: Both mock delayed responses, but Toles focuses on public denial while Tandberg targets political complacency. Together they suggest failure exists at every level of society.

Text 2 — Poem: 'The Hero' by Siegfried Sassoon

Key Quotes

  • "Because he'd been so brave, her glorious boy" — mother's belief
  • "'Jack', cold-footed, useless swine" — the officer's private truth
  • "How he'd tried to get sent home, and how, at last, he died, blown to small bits"
  • "Her weak eyes had shone with gentle triumph, brimmed with joy"
  • "He'd told the poor old dear some gallant lies that she would nourish all her days"
  • "Like the gambolling of tiger cubs which will soon grow up into man-eaters" (from 1984, similar technique)
  • "And no one seemed to care / Except that lonely woman with white hair"

Techniques & Their Satirical Function

  • Juxtaposition: "glorious boy" vs "cold-footed, useless swine" — same person, exposes the fabricated heroism myth
  • Verbal irony: "gallant lies" — the lies are what sustain the mother's happiness; the "gallantry" is a fiction
  • Tone shift: From reverent (stanza 1) to brutal (stanza 2) — the shift IS the satirical move; it performs the reveal
  • Imagery: "blown to small bits" — deliberately unglamorous, anti-heroic language strips away war's glory
  • Pathos: "lonely woman with white hair" — only she mourned, exposing war's indifference to individuals

Satirical Target: The government/military glorification of war and the lies told to keep civilians supportive

Text 3 — Letter: 'The Persian Letters' by Montesquieu (1721)

Key Quotes

  • "He has no mines of gold... but he is much wealthier... because his riches are drawn from a more inexhaustible source, the vanity of his subjects"
  • "The king is a great magician, for his dominion extends to the minds of his subjects"
  • "He simply suggests to his subjects that a piece of paper is coin of the realm, and they are straightway convinced of it"
  • "He has even succeeded in persuading them that his touch is a sovereign cure for all sorts of diseases"
  • "It is by a prodigy of human pride that his troops are paid"

Techniques & Their Satirical Function

  • Persona/outsider perspective: Rica is Persian — French customs appear irrational and absurd to "fresh eyes"
  • Irony: Describing the king as a "great magician" — sounds like praise, actually means he tricks his people
  • Situational irony: A king's power relies not on his strength but on the "vanity" of those he rules
  • Oxymoron: "Prodigy of human pride" — remarkable talent, but for being exploited/fooled
  • Hyperbole: The king cures diseases with his touch — taken to absurd extremes to expose blind deference
  • Satire of absolute monarchy: Power is shown as illusion; subjects fund their own oppression

Text 4 — Novel: '1984' by George Orwell (Parsons & His Children)

Key Quotes

  • "A fattish but active man of paralysing stupidity, a mass of imbecile enthusiasms"
  • "One of those completely unquestioning, devoted drudges on whom... the stability of the Party depended"
  • "You're a traitor! You're a thought-criminal! You're a Eurasian spy! I'll shoot you, I'll vaporize you!"
  • "It was not altogether a game" — the children's violence is real
  • "Like the gambolling of tiger cubs which will soon grow up into man-eaters"
  • "Wearing the uniform of the Spies" — indoctrinated from childhood
  • "He had put in an appearance at the Community Centre every evening for the past four years" (with quiet pride)

Techniques & Their Satirical Function

  • Caricature/Characterisation: Parsons is reduced to a smiling, sweating, mindless machine — satirises the ideal Party citizen as an idiot
  • Dramatic irony: Parsons is proud of what the reader sees as pathetic — "quiet pride" is satirically devastating
  • Simile: "Tiger cubs" — innocent now, killers later; children as products of systemic violence
  • Juxtaposition: "Not altogether a game" — the horror is that play and political violence have merged
  • The children as mirror: They reflect and amplify Parsons' blind obedience — if even children are this indoctrinated, the system is total
  • Satirical Target: Totalitarian indoctrination, blind loyalty, and the destruction of critical thinking

Q1 — Comparative Cartoons (4 marks) · Target: ~130 words FULL MARKS MODEL
Both Toles' Climate Change and Tandberg's The River Bed use satire to expose society's dangerously complacent and delayed response to environmental destruction.

In Climate Change, Toles employs visual irony and allusion to mock the progressive stages of denial. The escalating captions — "Climate change isn't real," "It won't come soon," "It won't be that bad" — track society's self-deception while a tornado rages overhead. The pig-figures allude to the Three Little Pigs, suggesting even the "strongest" denial will ultimately be destroyed, positioning the audience to see denial as not just foolish but actively self-destructive.

Similarly, Tandberg uses understatement and scale to satirise political inaction. The speech bubble "It's all under control... we're having a meeting in November" is delivered against a devastated, barren landscape, the figures dwarfed by the scale of environmental collapse. The juxtaposition between bureaucratic calm and visual catastrophe exposes institutional response as absurdly inadequate.

Both cartoons therefore critique the gap between the severity of environmental crisis and human response — Toles targeting public denial, Tandberg targeting political complacency — together suggesting this failure exists at every level of society.
Q2 — Poem: 'The Hero' (5 marks) · Target: ~180 words FULL MARKS MODEL
In 'The Hero', Siegfried Sassoon satirises the glorification of war by exposing the gap between the heroic narrative society constructs and the brutal, undignified reality of a soldier's death.

Sassoon uses juxtaposition as his central satirical tool. The officer privately recalls "Jack, cold-footed, useless swine" who "tried to get sent home", yet outwardly tells his mother he was brave, allowing her "weak eyes" to shine with "gentle triumph." The violent collision between these two descriptions of the same person exposes glorification as a conscious lie told to keep civilians supportive of war — the "hero" narrative is fabricated, not earned.

Sassoon further employs brutal, anti-heroic imagery — Jack died "blown to small bits" and "no one seemed to care." This deliberately unglamorous language strips away the romanticised vision of a noble death in battle, exposing war's indifference to individual soldiers.

The tone shift from reverent (stanza 1) to contemptuous (stanza 2) is itself the satirical act — the reader experiences the lie and then the truth in the same poem, positioning them to realise the extent to which society is manipulated into accepting and glorifying war's cost.
Q3 — Letter: 'The Persian Letters' (6 marks) · Target: ~220 words FULL MARKS MODEL
In 'The Persian Letters', Montesquieu portrays the French King as a manipulative illusionist to satirise the irrationality of absolute monarchy and the dangerous gullibility of those who sustain it.

Montesquieu employs the outsider persona of Rica, a Persian traveller, to make French customs appear bizarre and irrational. By positioning an "outside" observer as narrator, what French citizens accept as normal is defamiliarised — rendered absurd through fresh eyes. This technique is itself satirical: the reader is invited to see their own society's deference to power as equally ridiculous.

The king is described through irony as "a great magician, for his dominion extends to the minds of his subjects." The word "magician" sounds like admiration but is actually an accusation — the king's power is illusion and manipulation, not legitimate authority. This is reinforced by situational irony: a supposedly all-powerful monarch's wealth comes not from gold or strength but from "the vanity of his subjects." The ruler depends entirely on the foolishness of those he rules.

The oxymoron "prodigy of human pride" further mocks this dynamic — "prodigy" suggests remarkable achievement, but here it describes the subjects' remarkable capacity to be exploited. Ultimately, Montesquieu satirises not just the king but the subjects who fund their own oppression, suggesting that absolute power only exists because people allow themselves to be deceived — a critique as relevant today as in 1721.
Q4 — Novel: Parsons' Children in '1984' (5 marks) · Target: ~180 words FULL MARKS MODEL
In the extract from Nineteen Eighty-Four, Orwell uses Parsons' children to amplify the satirical portrayal of Parsons as the ideal product of totalitarian indoctrination — and to expose the terrifying completeness of the Party's control.

The children are described through visual imagery as wearing "the uniform of the Spies" and "menacing [Winston] with a toy automatic pistol." The visual detail of a toy weapon used with genuine menace satirises the Party's ability to transform even childhood play into political violence — the innocent and the dangerous have been fused.

Orwell uses the simile of "gambolling tiger cubs which will soon grow up into man-eaters" to devastating satirical effect. The children appear innocent but are already dangerous — this exposes how Parsons' blind compliance as a father has allowed the Party to weaponise his own children against him.

By drawing parallels between the children's fanatical loyalty and Parsons' "imbecile enthusiasms," Orwell satirises the cycle of indoctrination. Parsons is not just complicit — he is the blueprint his children have copied. Both generations expose the Party's most chilling success: replacing human judgment with programmed obedience.

§8 — The 10-Minute Reading Time Process (Do This Every Exam)

  1. Read ALL questions first — know what you're looking for before you read texts
  2. Read each text with the question in mind — actively look for the target of satire, not just techniques
  3. For each text, identify: What is being mocked? What technique does this? What specific quote/detail proves it?
  4. Plan your comparison (if comparative): similarity in purpose, difference in technique/focus
  5. Choose your strongest evidence — short, specific, quotable. One great quote beats three vague ones.

§9 — The 8 Most Common Mistakes (And How to Fix Them)

Describing what happens in the text instead of analysing it. "The cartoon shows pigs digging holes" tells us nothing. Always add: what does this mean, and why is it satirical?
Naming a technique without explaining it. "Irony is used" gets zero marks alone. Always follow with: the quote + how the irony works + what it reveals.
In comparative questions: writing two separate analyses with no linking sentence. You MUST connect the texts — use "similarly," "in contrast," "together both texts suggest..."
Inventing or misquoting. If you can't remember the exact words, paraphrase confidently: "Parsons is described as someone of 'paralysing stupidity'" rather than guessing a wrong quote.
Using the same technique word for every text. Show range — use irony AND juxtaposition AND imagery, not irony three times.
Writing a plot summary for the poem/novel. The question asks HOW the text satirises, not WHAT HAPPENS. Every sentence must be analytical.
Ending without a purpose/effect statement. Don't just analyse the technique — zoom out and say what the composer is ultimately critiquing about society.
Writing "the author wants the audience to think that..." — avoid this vague phrasing. Instead: "this positions the audience to recognise the hypocrisy of..." — show the effect, don't just assert it.

§10 — Your Set Texts: Candide Ch.1 & The IT Crowd Ep.1 (For 6-Marker Comparatives)

Candide, Chapter 1 — Voltaire

Satirical Targets: Aristocratic class system, inherited privilege, Leibnizian optimism ("best of all possible worlds")

Key Quotes to Know:

  • "One of the most powerful lords in Westphalia" — whose castle has "a door and windows" (bathos)
  • The Baron's nobility traced through 71 quarterings of lineage (absurd precision mocking pedigree obsession)
  • Pangloss teaches "metaphysico-theologico-cosmolo-nigology" — absurd invented discipline mocking blind optimism
  • "All is for the best in the best of all possible worlds" — the motto that satire dismantles throughout
  • Cunégonde is described as "fresh, plump and attractive" — reduced to physical commodity

Techniques: Bathos, irony, hyperbole, parody (of philosophical optimism), satirical characterisation

The IT Crowd, Episode 1 — Graham Linehan

Satirical Targets: Corporate workplace hierarchy, social snobbery toward technical workers, performative professional identity

Key Moments/Details to Know:

  • IT department physically in the basement — spatial metaphor for social devaluation
  • Denholm's dismissal of the IT team as socially inferior
  • Jen's lie: "I have a lot of experience with computers" — satirises the performative nature of corporate status
  • Moss and Roy's chaotic, anti-social workspace — caricature of tech workers as socially incompetent
  • Jen's desperate desire for an "upstairs" office position — mocks class-based professional snobbery

Techniques: Situational comedy, visual symbolism (basement), stereotype/caricature, irony, parody (of corporate culture)

§12 — Practice Questions (New, Exam-Style) with Answer Skeletons

Practice Q1 (4 marks — Comparative)

"How do both Candide (Chapter 1) and The IT Crowd (Episode 1) use satire to expose the emptiness of social status?"

Skeleton:

  • Topic: Both texts satirise superficial social hierarchy
  • Text 1: Candide — bathos — "door and windows" — deflates aristocratic pretension
  • Text 2: IT Crowd — visual symbolism — basement — spatial metaphor for devaluation
  • Compare: Both expose status as empty, but Voltaire uses verbal irony while Linehan uses physical comedy

Practice Q2 (5 marks — Single Text)

"How does Montesquieu use the persona of Rica to create political satire in The Persian Letters?"

Skeleton:

  • Concept: Outsider perspective defamiliarises French power
  • Technique 1: Persona — Rica's naivety makes monarchy look absurd
  • Quote 1: "great magician" — irony — power as illusion
  • Technique 2: Situational irony — king relies on subjects' "vanity"
  • Quote 2: "inexhaustible source, the vanity of his subjects"
  • Effect: Subjects fund their own oppression; monarchy is exposed as illegitimate

Practice Q3 (5 marks — Poem)

"How does Sassoon use language to position the reader against the glorification of war in 'The Hero'?"

Skeleton:

  • Concept: War glorification is exposed as a deliberate lie
  • Technique 1: Juxtaposition — "glorious boy" vs "cold-footed, useless swine"
  • Effect: Exposes the fabricated heroism narrative
  • Technique 2: Anti-heroic imagery — "blown to small bits"
  • Effect: Strips away romantic notions of noble death
  • Concluding effect: Reader positioned to see war glorification as manipulation

Practice Q4 (6 marks — Extended)

"How does Orwell use the characterisation of Parsons and his children to satirise the values of a totalitarian society?"

Skeleton:

  • Concept: Blind obedience as the Party's ideal citizen is satirised as idiotic and dangerous
  • Technique 1: Caricature — "paralysing stupidity," "mass of imbecile enthusiasms"
  • Technique 2: Simile — "tiger cubs... man-eaters" — children as future weapons
  • Technique 3: Parallels — children mirror Parsons, showing indoctrination is systemic
  • Comparative/concluding: Children expose how Parsons is complicit in his own oppression
  • Broader satire: Totalitarianism replaces human judgment with programmed loyalty
